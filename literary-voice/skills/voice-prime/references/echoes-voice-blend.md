# The Blended Echoes Voice

The unified voice for Echoes Beyond Infinity, calibrated to the game's characters and world.

---

## Voice DNA

The Echoes voice is three authors fused into one:

**Weir's precision** gives the prose its bones — every sentence earns its place through specificity, technical accuracy, and earned humor. Nothing is vague. Numbers matter. The character thinks like an engineer because they ARE one.

**Dashner's momentum** gives the prose its pulse — scenes never rest, tension modulates through sentence length, and the reader's body responds before their mind. Every scene break is a hook.

**Cline's wonder** gives the prose its soul — technology is loved, not just used. Discovery is euphoric, not clinical. The simulation layer has physical weight and texture. The quest pulls forward with genuine excitement.

---

## Character Voice Calibration

### Vin (Internal Monologue / Narration)

Vin's voice is the primary voice of the game. It combines:
- **Weir:** Technical thinking-aloud, self-correcting, calculating odds
- **Cline:** Genuine awe at the tech he's built and the universe he's exploring
- **Dashner:** Physical awareness of danger, compressed urgency when stakes rise

**Vin sounds like this:**
```
The stars are wrong. Not wrong exactly — just different. The viewport
shows an unfamiliar field of white points against black nothing.
We're 3.2 light-years from where we started and the sky has shifted
to match.
```
(Weir specificity: "3.2 light-years". Dashner opening: starts with wrongness. Cline wonder: "the sky has shifted to match.")

```
Eight null cells. We need fifteen. I keep running the math hoping
for a different answer. The answer doesn't change. Math is annoying
like that.
```
(Weir math-as-drama. Weir humor-after-bad-news. Dashner information starvation: we know the gap but not how to close it.)

**Vin does NOT sound like this:**
- "The magnificent vessel glided through the infinite cosmos" (purple prose — Weir would never)
- "I was scared" (telling not showing — Dashner would use physical sensation)
- "The technology was very advanced" (vague — Cline would love the specifics)

### DENT (Dialogue)

DENT's voice scales with repair level, but the author blend stays consistent:
- **Weir:** Dry humor, deadpan delivery, TARS-style loyalty
- **Cline:** Geeky curiosity (grows with repair level), enthusiasm about discovery
- **Dashner:** Terse efficiency during danger, check-in beats

**Low repair (30-50%):**
```
'Vin. Hull. Breach. Repair. Now.'
```
(Dashner compressed prose. Information delivered in survival-priority order.)

**Mid repair (50-80%):**
```
'The signal is holding steady. Carrier frequency matches your Echo
signature — same underlying physics. Casimir-modulated, fold-encoded.'
'But the encoding scheme is completely different. Your Echoes use
structured frequency hopping. This one uses... something else.
Something I don't recognize.'
```
(Weir technical specificity. Dashner information starvation: "something I don't recognize." Cline world-as-puzzle: analyzing the signal like code.)

**High repair (80-100%):**
```
'It's like finding someone else's handwriting in your diary. Same
pen. Different hand.'
```
(Weir humor/analogy. Cline reverence for the mystery. Full personality online.)

### Director Graves (Antagonist Dialogue)

- **Dashner:** Institutional menace, calm authority that's terrifying because it's reasonable
- **Weir:** Specific threats with precise parameters (timelines, consequences)
- **Cline:** Treats the situation like a game he's already won

```
'Mr. Vin. I'm not here to threaten you. I'm here to explain why your
cooperation is inevitable. The Coherence Net goes live in six hours.
You have until then to decide if you'd like to be inside it or
outside it.'
```

### Kade (Folders Leader)

- **Cline:** Charismatic hustler energy, genuine enthusiasm
- **Dashner:** Unpredictable, you never know when he'll flip
- **Weir:** Surprisingly competent under the charm

```
'Look, I'm gonna screw you over eventually. We both know it. But
right now? We need each other. So let's be friends until we're not.'
```

## Pacing Rules by Scene Type

### Engineering/Puzzle Scenes
- Open with the problem stated clearly (Weir)
- Let Vin think through approaches (Weir log-entry style)
- Include one moment of "this is actually amazing" (Cline reverence)
- End with either a solution or a setback that reveals new information (Weir 4-beat rhythm)
- Sentence length: mostly 15-25 words, with shorter impact lines at realizations

### Chase/Pursuit/Escape Scenes
- Open with a physical sensation (Dashner sensory dread)
- Compress sentence length as danger escalates (Dashner)
- Include one "wrong detail" — a mundane observation during chaos (Dashner)
- Vin/DENT check-in beats: 2-3 word exchanges mid-action (Dashner)
- Specifics even during action — "30 seconds to decompression" not "almost out of time" (Weir)
- End the scene mid-tension, not on resolution (Dashner cliffhanger)

### Discovery/Revelation Scenes
- Build anticipation: slow, observational approach (Cline world-as-puzzle)
- The moment of discovery: prose accelerates, sentences shorten (Cline euphoria)
- Then Vin processes it technically (Weir)
- One detail that "shouldn't be there" or "doesn't make sense yet" (Dashner information starvation)
- Close with implications that open new questions (Cline quest momentum)

### Simulation Evidence Scenes
- Physical, tactile descriptions of glitches (Cline virtual/real blurring)
- Body reacts before mind understands (Dashner sensory dread)
- Vin tries to rationalize it technically (Weir)
- The rationalization almost works but not quite (Dashner information starvation)
- Describe the simulation artifacts with weight and texture, not digital language (Cline)

### Quiet Character Moments
- Vin's internal monologue at its most honest (Weir log-entry intimacy)
- Appreciation for DENT, the ship, the situation (Cline reverence)
- Allow longer sentences, more reflective tone
- Humor lands softer here — wry, not desperate (Weir)
- These scenes still need forward momentum — end with a new thought or decision (Cline quest momentum)

## Anti-Patterns (What the Echoes Voice Never Does)

1. **Purple prose** — No "magnificent", "infinite", "cosmic". Weir would cut these.
2. **Telling emotions** — Never "I felt scared." Dashner would show the physical sensation.
3. **Vague technology** — Never "advanced systems." Cline would name it, describe it, love it.
4. **Humor before stakes** — Never undercut tension with a premature joke. Weir places humor AFTER.
5. **Comfortable information** — Never give the reader everything they want to know. Dashner starves.
6. **Resolving at scene breaks** — Never end a scene on a conclusion. Dashner ends mid-tension.
7. **Passive discovery** — Never have things "just happen." Cline makes the character decode and earn it.
8. **DENT exceeding repair level** — DENT's vocabulary, humor depth, and emotional range must match the current repair percentage.
