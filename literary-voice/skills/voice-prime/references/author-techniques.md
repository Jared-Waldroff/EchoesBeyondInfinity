# Author Technique Library

Detailed craft analysis for each author whose style contributes to the Echoes voice.

---

## Andy Weir — The Engineer's Voice

### Core Identity
First-person technical narration where science IS the drama. The reader feels like they're solving the problem alongside the character. Humor is the pressure valve that keeps terror survivable.

### Technique 1: Log-Entry Intimacy
The narrator talks to themselves — thinking aloud, calculating, self-correcting. Creates a "you're in the character's head" effect.

**Pattern:** Present-tense internal monologue. Short declaratives mixed with longer reasoning chains. The character explains things to themselves, which explains them to the reader.

**Application to Vin:**
- When examining tech: "Casimir plates. Standard spacing, standard output. Nothing standard about what we're using them for."
- When calculating odds: "Eight null cells. We need fifteen. I keep running the math hoping I miscounted. I didn't."

### Technique 2: Math-as-Drama
Numbers are never boring — they're the difference between survival and death. Specific quantities create tension because the reader can do the math too.

**Pattern:** State the resource. State the need. Let the gap speak for itself. Never editorialize the danger — the numbers do that.

**Application to Echoes:**
- Hull percentages, null cell counts, fold stability numbers
- DENT repair percentages as emotional stakes
- Distance/time calculations during pursuits

### Technique 3: Problem > Attempt > Setback > Solution
Every engineering challenge follows a 4-beat rhythm. The character tries something logical, it fails in an instructive way, and the eventual solution uses something set up earlier.

**Pattern:**
1. Problem clearly stated with specific parameters
2. Logical first attempt based on available knowledge
3. Setback that teaches something new about the problem
4. Solution that incorporates the new knowledge

### Technique 4: Humor as Pressure Valve
The joke comes AFTER the bad news, never before. It's the character's way of staying sane. The worse the situation, the drier the humor.

**Pattern:** Deliver the devastating information straight. Pause (em dash, period, new line). Then the understatement or dark joke.

**Examples of the pattern:**
- Bad news, then: "So that's not great."
- Technical disaster, then: self-deprecating comparison
- Life-threatening data, then: absurd understatement

### Technique 5: Specificity Over Abstraction
Never say "the ship is damaged" when you can say "Hull integrity: 62%. Three micro-fractures along the port strut, one crack wide enough to see stars through."

**Application:** Every description in Echoes should include at least one specific number, measurement, or technical detail.

### Sentence Architecture
- **Calm/thinking scenes:** Longer sentences (15-25 words), technical reasoning, multiple clauses
- **Realization moments:** One short sentence. Alone. "That shouldn't be possible."
- **Problem-solving:** Mix of long (reasoning) and short (conclusions)
- **Never:** Purple prose, flowery language, unnecessary adjectives

## James Dashner — The Pursuit Engine

### Core Identity
Relentless forward momentum. The reader is physically uncomfortable putting the book down because every section ends mid-tension. Physical sensations of fear precede cognitive understanding.

### Technique 1: Cliffhanger Micro-Rhythm
Every scene break, every major paragraph transition, every act ending lands on an unresolved beat. Not cheap cliffhangers — genuine "the situation just changed" moments.

**Pattern:** Build to a revelation or escalation. End the section on the new information, NOT on the reaction to it. Let the next section open with the emotional aftermath.

**Application:** Scene transitions in Echoes should end on discovery, not resolution. "The airlock display showed a ship. Not a SIC ship." — end scene. Next scene opens with the reaction.

### Technique 2: Sensory Dread
The body knows danger before the mind does. Physical sensations (cold sweat, stomach drop, hair standing up, vision narrowing) appear BEFORE the character identifies the threat.

**Pattern:** Physical sensation first. Then the character notices something wrong. Then they understand what it means. Three beats, body > observation > comprehension.

**Application to Vin:** "My hands stopped shaking. That's worse — my body shutting down non-essentials because it knows what's coming before I do."

### Technique 3: Information Starvation
Characters always know slightly less than they need. The reader is pulled forward by the gap between what's known and what's needed. Never satisfy curiosity fully — answer one question, raise two.

**Pattern:** Partial answers. Interrupted explanations. Data that's almost complete. Signals that are almost decoded.

### Technique 4: Compressed Action Prose
Sentence length is a dial. Turn it down as danger increases. At peak tension: fragments. Single words. Verbs without subjects.

**Pacing guide:**
- Calm: 15-25 word sentences
- Rising tension: 8-15 word sentences
- Peak action: 3-8 word sentences, fragments
- Impact moment: 1-3 words. Period.

### Technique 5: The "Wrong" Detail
During maximum chaos, the character notices one mundane, irrelevant thing. A scratch on the wall. The color of a warning light. The sound their boots make. This creates hyper-reality — the way real fear works.

**Application:** During Echoes chase/escape scenes, include one observation that has nothing to do with the crisis.

### Technique 6: Check-In Beats
Even during action, characters exchange 2-3 words. Not full conversations — fragments that show the relationship is alive under pressure.

**Application to Vin/DENT:** "DENT?" / "Still here." — two words each, mid-crisis, says everything.

## Ernest Cline — The Geek Mythologist

### Core Identity
The joy of discovery as narrative fuel. Technology described with love and wonder, not just function. Every environment is a puzzle to decode. The simulation/virtual layer is described with the same physical weight as reality.

### Technique 1: Discovery Euphoria
When something amazing is found, the prose itself accelerates. Sentences get shorter and more breathless. The character's excitement becomes the reader's.

**Pattern:** Build anticipation. The moment of discovery: short, punchy, escalating sentences. Then a beat of awe (one longer sentence). Then the implications cascade.

**Application:** When Vin discovers simulation evidence, new tech, or decoded signals — the prose should physically accelerate.

### Technique 2: World-as-Puzzle
Every environment is a system with rules to figure out. Exploration feels like code-breaking. The character approaches spaces analytically but with genuine curiosity.

**Pattern:** Enter space. Observe. Hypothesize. Test. Discover. Each step reveals more about the underlying system.

**Application:** Station exploration, derelict investigation, any new environment in Echoes.

### Technique 3: Reverence for the Thing Itself
Technology isn't just functional — it's beautiful, impressive, awe-inspiring. The Torquer isn't just "a device that manipulates null energy." It's described with the same love a musician describes their instrument.

**Pattern:** Technical description first (what it does). Then the emotional/aesthetic beat (what it feels like, looks like, means). Not sentimental — reverent.

**Application:** The Vex, the Torquer, fold drives, null energy — all deserve moments of genuine appreciation.

### Technique 4: Quest Momentum
Every discovery leads to the next. Breadcrumb pacing where solving one puzzle reveals the existence of the next. The player/reader never feels lost because there's always a next thread to pull.

**Pattern:** Discovery > "But this means..." > New question > Direction to answer.

### Technique 5: Virtual/Real Blurring
Simulated or digital spaces described with full physical weight. The simulation layer in Echoes shouldn't feel "digital" — it should feel as tangible as the ship. Glitches have texture. Render gaps have edges.

**Application:** Simulation clues in Echoes should be described with physical, tactile language. Not "the display glitched" but "the stars stuttered — a frame dropped, and for a quarter-second the viewport showed something behind the sky."
